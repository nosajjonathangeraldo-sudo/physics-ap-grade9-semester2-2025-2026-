Thermal Expansion & States of Matter

Thermal Expansion

Materials expand when heated because the particles inside them gain energy. As temperature increases, the particles vibrate faster and take up more space. This causes solids to get slightly longer, liquids to slightly expand in volume, and gases to expand a lot.

Linear Expansion

For long objects like rods or rails, expansion usually happens along the length. We calculate it using:

ΔL = α × L₀ × ΔT

Where:

  • ΔL = change in length (meters)
  • α = coefficient of linear expansion (1/°C, specific to material)
  • L₀ = original length (meters)
  • ΔT = temperature change (°C)

Step-by-Step Example 1

A steel rod is 3 meters long at 25°C. If the rod is heated to 75°C, and α = 0.000012/°C, calculate how much the rod length increases.

Step 1: Identify known values

  • L₀ = 3 m
  • ΔT = 75 − 25 = 50°C
  • α = 0.000012/°C

Step 2: Apply formula

ΔL = α × L₀ × ΔT
ΔL = 0.000012 × 3 × 50
ΔL = 0.0018 m

The steel rod expands by 0.0018 meters, which is 1.8 millimeters. Even though it seems small, this matters in bridges or railway tracks.

Step-by-Step Example 2

An aluminum rod of 2 m is heated from 20°C to 70°C. α for aluminum = 0.000024/°C. How much does it expand?

ΔT = 70 − 20 = 50°C
ΔL = 0.000024 × 2 × 50
ΔL = 0.0024 m = 2.4 mm

States of Matter

All substances exist as solid, liquid, or gas depending on how much energy the particles have:

Solid

Particles are tightly packed and vibrate in place. Solids have fixed shape and volume.

Example: Ice cube retains its shape in a glass. Only when heated to 0°C does it start melting.

Liquid

Particles move around freely but remain close. Liquids have fixed volume but shape changes to fit the container.

Example: Water poured into a bowl spreads to fill the bottom, but total volume remains the same.

Gas

Particles move freely and spread apart. Gases have no fixed shape or volume.

Example: Boiling water produces steam that fills the container completely.

Detailed Example

Suppose you have ice at −5°C and heat it gradually:

  1. −5°C to 0°C: solid ice warms but stays solid.
  2. 0°C: ice starts melting → solid changes to liquid.
  3. 0°C to 100°C: water heats → liquid expands slightly.
  4. 100°C: water boils → liquid changes to gas (steam) and expands rapidly.
